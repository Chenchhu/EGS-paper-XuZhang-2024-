A B S T R A C T


Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) is a promising approach to improve the production efficiency of deep geothermal energy (e.g. hot dry rock). However, it faces with significant unsolved challenges to create connected fracture networks between injection-production wells, maintain high flow rates and obtain sufficient thermal power in EGSs. To address these challenges, we propose a novel multilateral-well EGS method for improving the performance of both reservoir stimulation and heat extraction from hot dry rock. In this contribution, we construct an integrated numerical model to simulate the processes of complex fracture propagation and thermal heat production as a whole. This model fully couples fluid flow, heat transfer, rock deformation and fracture propagation, taking into account the generation of new fractures, reactivation of pre-existing fractures, and mechanical interaction between hydraulic and natural fractures. The reliability of the integrated model is well verified with the analytical solution and experimental data. Subsequently, the damage and temperature fields as well as the resulting heat extraction performance of the multilateral-well EGS and conventional vertical-well EGS are compared. The results indicate that the stimulated reservoir permeability and connectivity, extracted thermal power under the configuration of multilateral-well EGS are much higher (about an order of magnitude) than those under the scheme of vertical-well EGS. This study presents a promising alternative for EGS to achieve enhanced reservoir stimulation and heat extraction performance.

1. Introduction


Geothermal energy emerges as a promising alternative to fossil fuels, attributed to its substantial and renewable reserves [1-3]. Currently, Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) is preferred for the development of deep geothermal resources, specifically those with low porosity and low permeability, such as Hot Dry Rocks (HDRs) [4-8]. In the context of EGS approach, a pair of vertical wells are drilled, with one serving as an injection well and the other as a production well. These wells are used to inject large-volume fluids into the subsurface formation for creating an artificial heat reservoir (Fig. 1a) [9-13]. Its main purpose is to generate abundant fracture surfaces and gain improved reservoir connectivity for fluid flow and heat transfer [9-13]. The world has witnessed the establishment of more than 60 EGS projects globally. However, the majority of these projects have ceased operations, far from achieving full-scale commercialization [14-17]. The primary reasons for shutting down these EGS projects are the inability to effectively improve fracture stimulation and enhance heat extraction, manifested as the formation of a singular disconnected fracture between injection-production wells, resulting in inefficient fluid injection and heat transportation, ultimately leading to low geothermal extraction efficiency (Fig. 1a) [14].

To address these unsolved challenges, we introduce a pioneering methodology aimed at optimizing reservoir stimulation and heat extraction within EGSs through the utilization of multilateral well systems (Fig. 1b). Its objective centers around enhancing reservoir permeability and connectivity, thus improving fluid circulation and heat transport. For the multilateral-well EGS method, it encompasses the initial drilling of two principal wellbores (i.e. vertical wells) within HDRs, followed by the implementation of either Radial Jet Drilling (RJD) or Coiled-Tubing Drilling (CTD) technologies to create multilateral wells stemming from the main wellbores in different directions and depths (Fig. 2a) [18-22]. The lateral extensions of these multilateral wells span up to 100m through RJD and up to 2000m via CTD, accompanied by diameters ranging from 0.05 to 0.12m [18,19,23]. Further detailed insights into multilateral well drilling technologies can be found in Refs [19-22,24]. Finally, cold working fluid is injected from the injection well and flows through the aforementioned artificial fracture networks to absorb heats from such high-temperature and stimulated fractured domain. The heated working fluid is consequently extracted from the production well for power generation (Fig. 2c). In 2008, RJD technology is utilized to drill 12 multilateral wells (each spanning approximately 40m in length) from an injection well located at the Klaipéda geothermal field, which results in a substantive enhancement in injectivity (approximately 14% ) [23]. Furthermore, it is of central importance to investigate and clarify three key questions regarding the multilateral-well EGS: (i) are more artificial fractures generated? In this study, we develop a comprehensive numerical model that integrates the simulation of both stimulation and production processes within fractured geothermal reservoirs. We treat the fractured geothermal reservoir as a combination of rock matrix and embedded fractures. The mechanisms that are of great importance for hydraulic fracturing and heat extraction of EGS are taken into consideration, such as thermal-hydraulic-mechanical coupling effects, generation of new fractures, reactivation of pre-existing fractures and interplay between stimulated and natural fractures. Subsequently, we apply this model to investigate and evaluate the performance of hydraulic stimulation and thermal production in fractured geothermal reservoirs, where multilateral wells are employed. Our findings strongly support the viability of multilateral-well EGS as a promising alternative for achieving enhanced reservoir stimulation and efficient heat extraction in HDRs. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In section 2, to study the stimulation and production processes in a fractured geothermal reservoir, a Thermal-Hydraulic-Mechanical-Damage (THMD) coupling model is constructed. In section 3, the reliability of the integrated model is well verified with the analytical solution and experimental data. In section 4, the model setup for simulating fluid injection and production from a deep fractured geothermal reservoir is described, which includes two cases, i.e. the vertical-well EGS and multilateral-well EGS. This is followed by the presentation and analysis of simulation results in section 5 . In section 6, the paper ends with a brief conclusion.

2. Methodology


In this paper, we perform a numerical study to imitate the processes of reservoir stimulation and heat extraction in a fractured geothermal reservoir. We model the fracture network embedded in a solid matrix obeying a power law scaling. The thermo-poro-elastic effect and elasto-brittle constitutive relation are employed to characterize the spatiotemporal evolution of pressure variation, temperature evolution, stress alteration, and damage propagation within fractured geothermal reservoirs. We present a detailed description of the governing equations of our numerical model for describing natural fracture networks (section 2.1), and computing fluid flow (section 2.2.1), heat transfer (section 2.2.2), rock deformation and damage (section 2.2.3).

2.1. Fracture network model


Fracture systems often display a diverse range of fracture lengths (e. g. ranging from millimeter to kilometer), which is often described through a power-law statistical model [30-32]. This model is defined by a density function expressed as follows [30,31]:

(1)n(l)=αl−a,forl∈[lmin,lmax]

where l represents the fracture length, a signifies the power-law length exponent and α denotes the density term. It is noteworthy that within this model, the most significant characteristic length scales are the minimum (lmin) and maximum (lmax) fracture lengths. When conducting numerical simulations, L is the size of the modeling domain,with the condition that lmin≪L≪lmax usually holds. Based on extensive outcrop data,the value of power-law length exponent a typically falls within the range of 1.5-3.5 [30].

The parameter of fracture intensity,denoted as γ ,signifies the cumulative length of fractures per unit area. It has a direct connection with the initial moment of the intensity distribution for fracture lengths, expressed as:

(2)γ=1L2∫n(l,L)ldl

where l ’ represents the fracture length considered within the modeling domain of area AL=L2 .

The fully-coupled THMD model is implemented within the framework of the finite element method in COMSOL. It is solved through the use of an implicit generalized alpha time-dependent solution approach. The spatial domain has been discretized employing an unstructured mesh (consisting of triangular finite elements). We have validated the THM coupling model by simulating the cold fluids injecting into a high-temperature reservoir, with a particular focus on the variations of rock temperature, pore pressure, and stress state (in section 3.1). Furthermore, we have conducted an assessment of the model's accuracy in simulating the propagation of hydraulically-induced fractures, with a comparison against an analytical solution under the condition of a viscosity-dominated regime (in section 3.2). Additionally, the model's representation of the mechanical interaction between hydraulic and natural fractures has been validated through comparisons with experimental data (in section 3.3).

3. Model validation


3.1. Validation of the model for simulating coupled thermo-hydro- mechanical processes


The validation of such coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical model involves simulating changes over time in rock temperature, pore pressure, and stress conditions, resulting from a sudden temperature reduction in a hot reservoir around a wellbore. Fig. 4 illustrates the model's configuration, depicting initial and boundary conditions. Our simulation framework is adapted from the work of Ghassemi and Zhang [41]. It entails a 0.1−m -diameter wellbore within an infinite granite reservoir, with initial reservoir temperature and pore pressure set at 200∘C and 0 MPa, respectively. Subsequently, the wellbore experiences a rapid temperature drop to 80∘C while maintaining a pore pressure of 0MPa . The wellbore boundary conditions are defined as free in response to stress variations, with the outer stress, pore pressure, and temperature boundaries specified as σx∞=σy∞=p∞=0MPa and T∞=200∘C . In Fig. 5, a comparison is presented between the numerical and analytical solutions for the rock temperature (Fig. 5a), pore pressure (Fig. 5b), tangential stress (Fig. 5c), and radial stress (Fig. 5d) along the radial direction away from the wellbore at various time intervals (i.e. t=103 , 104,105,106s ). The results demonstrate a high degree of concordance between the numerical and analytical solutions, thus affirming the accuracy of coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical model and the fidelity of numerical implementation.

3.2. Validation of fluid-driven fracture propagation


In this section, we embark on the verification of the fluid injection-induced fracturing process through the utilization of a hydraulic fracture propagation model primarily governed by the viscosity regime, known as the KGD model. To assess the accuracy of our numerical model (i.e. comparing numerical results with analytical solutions), we employ a first-order approximation of the fracture half-length, as previously derived in the work of Detournay [42]. A planar strain model with dimensions of 100m×100m is meticulously established. Within this model, the stress conditions have been defined including a maximum horizontal stress of 90MPa ,a minimum horizontal stress of 45MPa ,and a pore pressure of 30MPa . water-based fracturing fluid is consistently injected at a rate of 2×10−4m2/s . Additionally,the material properties of the rock have been defined, encompassing an elastic modulus of 60 GPa, a Poisson's ratio of 0.25 , a tensile strength of 10 MPa, and a fluid viscosity of 10mPa s. It is imperative to emphasize that the assumption of homogeneity in the hydraulic-mechanical properties of the rock is maintained.

Fig. 6 illustrates the initiation and propagation of a fluid-driven fracture originating from the injection point. The fracture extends both left and rightward, following the direction of the maximum horizontal principal stress. The half-length of the fracture, extracted from the damage results at various time points, exhibits a strong alignment with the first-order approximation. This alignment serves as compelling evidence for the precision and viability of our numerical method in predicting fracture propagation.

3.3. Validation of interaction between hydraulic and natural fractures


In the context of hydraulic fracturing withing a fractured geological domain, distinct propagation behaviors are observed, manifested as "cross" and "arrest". Therefore, it is crucial to validate the accuracy of these propagation behaviors when hydraulic fractures approach a weak plane in a fractured reservoir. To achieve this, we construct a numerical model based on the relevant experimental study [42]. The numerical model,sized at 300mm×300mm ,features a wellbore of 6mm diameter, and incorporates two natural fractures. Water injection originates from the wellbore. Normal constraints are applied to the left and lower sides of the model. On the upper and right sides, we apply the maximum and minimum horizontal principal stresses, respectively (Fig. 7a). We take great care to align the parameters in the numerical simulation as closely as possible with those outlined in the referenced literature [43,44] . Table 1 shows the simulation scenarios associated with various intersection angle between hydraulic and natural fractures, and stress differential between maximum and minimum principal stresses.

Fig. 7b visually presents the interplay between the hydraulic fracture and natural fractures associated with different intersection angle and stress difference. It can be observed that "cross" or "arrest" behavior predominantly hinges on the intersection angle and stress differential. Larger intersection angle and higher stress differential encourage direct crossing. Conversely, reduced intersection angles and stress differentials inhibit direct crossing, culminating in the arrest of hydraulic fractures by the natural fracture. The comparison between numerical and experimental results [44] demonstrates a strong agreement in fracture interaction processes (Fig. 8). These findings confirm the effectiveness of the constructed fracture propagation model in representing the interactions between hydraulic fractures and natural fractures in the context of hydraulic fracturing procedures.

4.1. Computational model


The orientation of multilateral well is perpendicular to the direction of maximum principal stress (Fig. 9a). This is because hydraulic fractures tend to propagate along the path of least resistance (e.g. the path along the direction of maximum principal stress), which would also enhance the intersection probability between artificial fracture networks and injection-production wells. The setup of a two-dimensional numerical model (Fig. 9b) encompassed a 100m×100m area is the horizontally placed domain derived from Fig. 9a, which includes a fractured rock mass and an auxiliary domain. 4.2. Description of well arrangements


This study involves the comparative analysis of fracture propagation and heat production performances within three distinct well layout schemes, each represented in Fig. 10. The three well arrangements comprise one scheme of conventional vertical-well EGS (Fig. 10a) and two types of multilateral-well EGS, i.e. reservoir stimulation through open hole fracturing (Fig. 10b) or four stages fracturing (Fig. 10c). Each EGS system encompasses two wells, which are used for injecting large-volume fluids to conduct hydraulic fracturing. For the configuration of vertical-well EGS, the two vertical wells are located at the center of the upper and lower boundaries of the fractured rock mass. These wells simulate perforation hydraulic fracturing, with one perforation point per vertical well. The perforation direction is parallel to the maximum horizontal principal stress direction of the geological formation, with 1 m of perforation depth. For multilateral-well EGS with four stages fracturing, each multilateral well has four perforation points and the spacing between each perforation point is 10m . Other parameters are consistent with that of the vertical-well EGS.

4.3. Initial and boundary conditions


The geological data from the FORGE (Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy) site provides crucial information about the conditions of a potential fractured geothermal reservoir [45]. Table 2 lists the material properties of the matrix block, fractures, and working fluids. The temperature within a potential EGS reservoir at the FORGE site can range from 175 to 225∘C . Stress magnitudes are specified with a minimum horizontal principal stress (σh) ranging from 13.1 to 14.2kPa/m and a maximum horizontal principal stress (σH) ranging from 15.4 to 18.5kPa/m . Thus,the maximum and minimum horizontal principal stresses (for the geothermal reservoir buried at depth of 3km ) are specified as 55MPa and 40MPa ,respectively. Stress is applied orthogonally to the model. Additionally, at the base of the model, a roller constraint is imposed,preventing displacement in the y -direction, as illustrated in Fig. 9b. In the subsurface region, within the fractured rock structure and before any injection or production processes, an in-situ water pressure is maintained at 25MPa . The outer boundaries of the system are subject to constraints of no-flow and heat insulation. The initial temperature of the reservoir is uniformly set at 200∘C for both the rock matrix and the water. The temperature of the injection well is maintained at 60∘C . These established parameters and conditions are consistently applied throughout both the reservoir stimulation and heat extraction stages. During the EGS fracturing phase, water is injected into the system at a constant rate of q=1×10−4m2/s . Injection continues until fractures propagate to the boundary of the fractured domain. Following the stimulation phase, the obtained stimulation results are employed as the initial condition for modeling a comprehensive heat extraction process. This modeling spans a duration of 1800 days. To facilitate fluid circulation within the fractured rock mass, a constant pressure differential is maintained between the inlet and outlet. Specifically, pressures at the injection and production wells are fixed at 28 MPa and 25 MPa, respectively.

4.4. Workflow of numerical simulations


Two consecutive modeling stages are involved in the simulation, i.e. reservoir stimulation and heat production, where the reservoir stimulation results are specified as the initial conditions for modeling the process of heat production. The spatial discretization of the model employs an unstructured grid with an average element size of 0.5m , determined as a fraction of the model’s characteristic length (i.e. L/100). The time-domain computation is managed by an implicit solver operating with adaptive stepping. It adheres to a maximum time step Δtmax =0.01h and maintains a tolerance of 0.01 to regulate relative errors during the stage of reservoir stimulation. This approach ensures numerical convergence and computational stability. During each time step, the solver iterates multiple times (up to a predefined maximum limit of 40 iterations) to minimize relative errors until they are below the specified tolerance. After successful convergence, the solver advances to the subsequent time step. In cases where the tolerance criterion is not met, the solver takes an automatic step to reduce the time step by half and reiterates the process until convergence is achieved. If the tolerance limit continues to be exceeded, the time step is further reduced. Notably, during the initial phase of simulating fluid injection, the time step starts at an extremely small value of Δtini =1×10−6h . Subsequently,this time step is adjusted iteratively, either increased or reduced, based on convergence conditions while ensuring that it remains below the maximum time step (Δtmax) .

5. Results and analysis


5.1. Reservoir stimulation characteristic


Fig. 11 presents the spatial and temporal evolution of damage and pressure within the geological domain during the fluid injection phase of vertical-well EGS. At the initial injection time (e.g. t=0.1h ),the injection of water into the fractured rock triggers a rapid escalation in fluid pressure within the reservoir connected to the injection point. This heightened fluid pressure serves as the catalyst for the initiation of hydraulic fractures, predominantly propagating in alignment with the maximum principal stress (i.e. along the y -axis). It is noteworthy that the matrix experiences limited water diffusion due to its inherent low permeability, resulting in the concentration of elevated pressure within the locally connected fracture cluster. As the injection process continues (t=0.6h) ,the cracks persistently propagate,progressively linking with preexisting fractures which are originally isolated from the injection point. Finally, these newly connected fractures again serve as conduits for the migration of high-pressure fluid and the lengthened fractures extend out of the domain (t=2.4h) .



Fig. 11. (a) Pressure evolution, and (b) damage propagation in the fractured rock during fluid injection. The Red line in the visual representation represents by draulic fractures that are expanding within the solid matrix. The blue line represents hydraulic fractures that are extending along pre-existing natural fractures.

In the visual representation of damage propagation (i.e. Fig. 11b), the red line denotes newly induced fractures within the rock matrix, while the blue line depicts hydraulic fractures that extend along preexisting natural fractures (i.e. reactivation of pre-existing fractures). When hydraulic fractures interact with pre-existing natural fractures, they have the potential to either penetrate through, propagate along, or divide along pre-existing natural fractures. As a result, the hydraulic fracture propagation path deviates from a planar trajectory. This deviation arises from the interplay between two competing forces that govern the overall direction of hydraulic fracture propagation. One force drives the fracture to follow the weakened planes formed by the preexisting natural fractures, while the other force compels it to propagate along the direction of maximum principal stress. Because the directions of natural fracture sets are not aligned with that of in-situ principal stresses, so the stimulated fracture must balance these two forces and minimize the fracturing energy to locate the propagation path possessing the least resistance. This explains the observed zig-zag pattern of hydraulic fracture propagation. Eventually, these factors contribute to the formation of a no-planar fracture network, which disconnects with the injection and production wells when conducting hydraulic fracturing operations through the vertical-well EGS.

Fig. 12 presents the temporal evolution of newly induced damage in the fractured rocks and the fluid pressure at the injection well. We examine the emergence of damages within the fractured mass by analyzing the area of newly damaged rocks during each 0.01−h time interval throughout the 2.4-h injection period. Simulation results reveal a strong intrinsic relationship between burst-like pressure fluctuations and the presence of a complex network of natural fractures. When the hydraulic fracture interacts with a natural fracture, a consequential drop in pressure occurs (manifested as some peaks of speckles), due to the increased fluid storage capacity resulting from the activation of natural fractures. Subsequently, within a finite period, water primarily flows into the reactivated pre-existing natural fracture clusters instead of propagating new damages.

5.2. Heat extraction performance


When it comes to the production phase of EGS, we perform heat extraction modeling based on the fracturing stimulation results, in which the most important component is the stimulated fracture trajectory. The wells located at the bottom and upper sides of the fractured domain (referring to the reservoir stimulation phase, Fig. 10a) are converted to the injection well (i.e. the point marked in blue) and production well (i.e. the point marked in red), respectively (Fig. 13a). The heat extraction performance is analyzed from various perspectives, including the flow velocity distribution (Fig. 13d), the equivalent rock mass permeability (Fig. 14), the local temperature distribution (Fig. 15a) and the overall thermal characteristics (Fig. 16). In the scenario of vertical-well EGS with one injection and one production well (Fig. 13a), the flow velocity is extremely low (Fig. 13d) because the fluid flow is hindered by the low-permeability matrix embedded with isolating fractures between injection-production wells (notably, regions characterized by heightened fluid velocities correspond to fractures that interconnect with injection and production wells within the EGS). The flow capacity (i.e. keq  ) remains similar and insufficient compared to the pre-stimulation state (i.e. keq  is small as depicted in Fig. 14). The thermal front propagates uniformly, with temperature variation primarily concentrated around the injection well (Fig. 15a). Consequently, the average production temperature is high and remains almost the same as the initial reservoir temperature of 200∘C (Fig. 16). However,the global thermal power is notably low (Fig. 16). The abovementioned results suggest that when fractures stimulated during reservoir stimulation stage cannot connect with both injection-production wells, the stimulated fractures are useless for effectively enhancing fluid flow and heat extraction performance during the production phase of EGS.

Therefore, in order to use the stimulated fractures for generating effective flow channels between injection and production wells, it is supposed to drill directional wells to connect artificial fractures and thus transitioning the connectivity of wells-fractures form a disconnected state to an interconnected regime. To this end, one (or two) directional wells is constructed manifested as Fig. 13b and c. Fig. 13 presents the well configuration employed during the production phase of EGS (i.e. after conducting reservoir stimulation) and the distribution of fluid velocities within fractured geological formations. Regarding vertical-well EGS configuration featuring two injection and two production wells (Fig. 13c and f),two dominant flow channels exist between the inlet and outlet, exhibiting rapid flow velocity. Conversely, other disconnected fractures manifest substantially diminished fluid velocities. For the scheme of vertical-well EGS with one injection and two production wells (Fig. 13b and e), the system is dominated by one preferential flow path for high fluid migration since only a single channel remains interconnected between injection and production wells. In contrast, for the other hydraulically generated fracture (i.e. the one situated on the right in Fig. 13b and e) are mostly restrained with inadequate fluid movement. Fig. 14 shows the correlation between the equivalent permeability keq  of a stimulated fractured reservoir and various well patterns employed for the production stage of EGS. As the number of vertical wells increases (i.e. transitioning from a configuration with one injection well and one production well to a setup with two injection wells and two production wells), stimulated fracture networks becomes connecting to the inlet and outlet, keq  experiences a significant increment. This is attributed to the fact that more flow pathways are engaged in migrating fluid and high-permeability fractures connecting injection-production wells start to dominate the flow (Fig. 13d, e and f).

Fig. 15 illustrates the temperature distribution and evolution within fractured geothermal reservoirs across various configurations of vertical-well EGS. When interconnected fracture networks are established, as observed in a fractured rock mass with one injection well and two production wells (Fig. 15b), thermal coning occurs along a few connected fractures accompanied by low-temperature regions (Fig. 15b). As the system incorporates more production wells, the low-temperature zone expands broadly (Fig. 15c), since more fluid circuits are engaged and contribute to heat exchange (Fig. 13f). In terms of the temporal evolution of the average outlet temperature and global thermal power during EGS production, the thermal breakthrough (i.e. the production temperature starts to decrease) occurs early in the production stage, accompanied by a substantial increase in global thermal power (Fig. 16). In this contribution, drilling directional wells to connect stimulated fractures is a feasible method for enhancing heat production performance in EGS, which signifies the great importance of establishing connected stimulated fractures between injection and production wells.

5.3. Comparisons between multilateral-well EGS and vertical-well EGS


In this section, we examine the performance of reservoir stimulation and heat extraction under the scheme of multilateral-well EGS. As for the system of multilateral-well EGS, reservoir stimulation is conducted through the approach of open-hole fracturing and segmented fracturing (Fig. 10b and c). On the basis of stimulation consequences, we simulate the thermal production performance, in which the upper and bottom sides of fractured rock mass are treated as production and injection wells respectively (Fig. 18b and c). In comparison to vertical-well fracturing, multilateral-well EGS with open-hole fracturing exhibits a higher propensity for generating new fractures or activating natural ones (as evidenced in Fig. 17a and b) and enhances the connectivity between stimulated fractures and wellbores (Fig. 18d and e). Such enhanced fracture areas and improved reservoir connectivity arise from the adoption of multilateral wells, which increases contact areas between the well and reservoir, and raises the likelihood of hydraulically stimulated fractures connecting with wellbores. However, it is worth noting that multilateral well open-hole fracturing typically generates a limited number of connected fractures (Fig. 17b and 18e), because fractures induced by high pressure fluid injection tend to propagate along the preferential path of least resistance. Once the preferential fracture extends to another well, the generation of new fractures becomes quite challenging. To overcome this limitation, a segmented fracturing approach is proposed for multilateral wells (Fig. 10c). It can be seen that multiple high-flow-rate hydraulic fractures are generated, accompanied by the enhanced connectivity with both injection and production wells (Fig. 18c and f). Fig. 19 provides insights into the variation of equivalent rock mass permeability keq in relation to various well patterns. The flow capacity (represented by equivalent permeability) is most pronounced in the case of multilateral well segmented fracturing, followed by multilateral well open-hole fracturing, and is weakest in vertical well hydraulic fracturing. Notably, the equivalent permeability of the fractured reservoir after multilateral well segmented fracturing (i.e. 4.07× 10−15m2 ) is approximately two orders of magnitude higher than that of vertical well hydraulic fracturing with one injection and one production wells (i.e. 1.64×10−17m2 ),underscoring the significant advantage of the scheme of multilateral-well EGS.

When it comes to the thermal production performance associated with the scenario of vertical well EGS (Fig. 20a), most heats are remained within the fractured geothermal reservoirs resulting from the stimulated fractures disconnected with injection-production wells. Conversely, for all multilateral well patterns, stimulated fracture sets are able to connect with the inlet and outlet (Fig. 18e and f),serving as the dominant channels for heat transfer such that more heats are extracted around the percolated fracture clusters. This is due to the fact that the heat extraction performance in a geothermal reservoir is controlled by the heat convection behavior rather than the heat conduction process, whereas the heat convection behavior is governed by the permeability-determined fluid velocity along flow pathways. In addition, more heats are extracted from the multilateral-well segmented fracturing scheme as compared to the multilateral-well open-hole fracturing scenario (Fig. 20b and c).

Fig. 21 shows the evolution of heat extraction performance (i.e. production temperature and global thermal power) with respect to various well arrangements. For the vertical-well case, production temperature maintains at a high and stable level due to the insufficient heat exchange between cold fluids and hot reservoirs. In contrast, as to the multilateral-well layout, the stable stage of production temperature becomes shorter and its temperature drop displays much faster resulting from the enhanced production flow rate (Fig. 18e and f). However, the enhanced production flow rate ultimately makes the global thermal power extracted from the multilateral-well system (i.e. 3.92×1011J/m for the open-hole fracturing case and 7.59×1011J/m for the segmented fractured scenario) much larger than that of the vertical-well one, approaching to 1.6-3.1 folds at the end of thermal production stage for the vertical-well EGS with one injection and two production wells (i.e. 2.45×1011J/m as depicted in Fig. 16).

6. Conclusions


In this work, we introduce an innovative methodology through the employment of multilateral wells to improve the performance of reservoir stimulation and heat extraction within EGSs. In addition, we develop a fully coupled thermal-hydraulic-mechanical-damage model to simulate the processes of fracturing stimulation and heat production with a seamless connection. The proposed model integrates critical mechanisms associated with stimulation and circulation, including the reactivation of isolated and/or hydraulically connected natural fractures, generation of newly stimulated fractures, mechanical interaction between artificial and pre-existing fractures, and thermal-hydraulic-mechanical coupling effects. The developed comprehensive model is verified by comparing numerical results against analytical solution and experimental data. Using the developed and validated model, we systematically investigate the operation processes for EGS reservoirs under the configurations of vertical-well EGS and multilateral-well EGS. It is demonstrated that the multilateral-well EGS scheme is a promising alternative for developing hot dry rock to achieve enhanced reservoir stimulation and heat extraction performance. The main conclusions are summarized as follows.

(1) The interaction between artificial and natural fractures in geothermal reservoirs leads to various complex behaviors, including termination, direct crossing, or offset crossing. Such interaction behaviors are influenced by two opposing forces: One force drives hydraulic fractures to follow the direction of weak planes manifested as reactivation of natural fracture sets. The second force compels hydraulic fractures to propagate along the direction of maximum principal stress and open against the minimum principal stress. The hydraulic fracture must propagate through balancing these two forces and minimizing the fracturing energy to find the extended path with least resistance. To this end, hydraulic fractures often take a zig-zag path across the fractured geothermal reservoir.

(2) As to the stage of reservoir stimulation, non-planer cracks are preferred to be created under the scheme of vertical-well EGS, which is detrimental to form connected fractures between injection and production wells. When conducting heat extraction from EGSs based on the stimulated unconnected fracture network, fluid flow velocity is extremely low and the heat transfer process is dominated by heat conduction, leading to the resulting cold fluid swept area is limited and the global amount of heat extracted from hot reservoirs is tiny. Such integrated stimulation and production phenomena highlight the disadvantage of using vertical wells to develop EGSs. In order to conquer these constraints, we put forward a remedial measure through changing the procedures of vertical well drilling and hydraulic fracturing. Firstly, one vertical well (i.e. injection well) can be drilled to conduct the process of hydraulic fracturing, and then according to the location of stimulated fracture network, it is supposed to design the trajectory of another directional well (i.e. production well) to communicate with the generated fracture network. Ultimately, interconnected flow paths between injection and production wells can be formed and utilized to extract heats from deep geothermal reservoirs.

(3) Stimulation and production operations under the scenario of multilateral-well EGS can be an effective method to tap deep geothermal energy. In contrast to traditional vertical well hydraulic fracturing, the proposed approach manifests three notable advantages. Firstly, it demonstrates a remarkable capability for generating a higher density of fractures within the reservoir. Secondly, this innovative technique excels in establishing seamless communication channels between fractures and both injection and production wells without the need for additional well drilling. Lastly, by the promoted development of larger fractures and bolstered interconnectivity between fractures and wells, cold fluids swept areas and heat transfer efficiency are substantially amplified.

However, there are still some limitations existed in this study. First, this work does not consider the occurrence of seismic activities in geothermal reservoirs associated with the stress perturbation triggered by reservoir stimulation [46-48]. Second, it is of great interest to conduct a systematic investigation to quantify how different properties of realistic fracture networks (e.g. fracture length, density, orientation and percolation parameter) affect the stimulation and production processes in naturally fractured reservoirs. In addition, future work may also be needed to explore more complicated scenarios of reservoir stimulation and heat extraction, such as three-dimensional fractured geothermal reservoirs, which however can be computationally very expensive to run.
